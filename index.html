  • SSCI Wang, J., & Zhou, J. (under review). Revisiting posthumanism in language studies: Twenty years on (2006–2025). Language Sciences. Abstract ▾

    This study presents a comprehensive bibliometric analysis of posthumanism-related research in linguistic studies published between 2006 and 2025. Drawing on 151 articles retrieved from the Web of Science (SSCI) database, the analysis employs CiteSpace to map the intellectual structure, temporal evolution, influential contributors, key publication journals, and major research hotspots within this emerging field. Situated against the backdrop of accelerating technological development, ecological crisis, and growing scholarly critiques of anthropocentric paradigms, the study examines how posthumanist perspectives have been increasingly integrated into language research. The results reveal a clear shift from marginal engagement prior to 2018 toward rapid expansion after 2019, corresponding with the rise of artificial intelligence, multimodal communication, and ecological concerns. The United States, England, Canada, and Australia emerge as central knowledge hubs, while scholars such as Rosi Braidotti, Karen Barad, and Alastair Pennycook play pivotal roles in shaping the theoretical and methodological foundations of the field. Keyword co-occurrence and cluster analysis further identify three dominant research hotspots: “generative AI”, “climate change denial”, and “new materialism”. By quantitatively mapping the development of posthumanist linguistics, this study not only clarifies the field’s intellectual landscape but also highlights its growing significance in reconfiguring language, agency, and meaning-making in a more-than-human world.

  • SSCI Wang, J., & Zhou, J. (under review). Powerless, passive, and paralyzed individuals facing the surging tide of AI technology: A corpus-based analysis of media discourse on Artificial Intelligence through the lens of Proximization Theory. Language Sciences. Abstract ▾

    This study employs Proximization Theory to analyze how U.S. media discourse constructs AI as an imminent and morally charged threat. Through corpus-based critical discourse analysis of 63 CNN articles (2023-2025), the research identifies strategic use of spatial, temporal, and axiological proximization. Findings reveal that media representations frame AI as an external force encroaching upon human domains, compress its impact into an urgent timeline, and polarize values to evoke ideological conflict. This construction naturalizes technological inevitability, grammatically erases human agency, manufactures moral panic via existential risk framing, and privileges expert voices. Consequently, the discourse interpellates individuals as powerless spectators and constrains public deliberation over AI’s societal integration.

  • SSCI Wang, J., & Zhou, J. (under review). The passive, muted, and powerless public in the controversial AI revolution: A systemic-functional linguistic analysis of media representation of AI in news media. IEEE Transactions on Professional Communication. Abstract ▾

    This study employs Systemic Functional Linguistics, specifically transitivity analysis and the appraisal model, to critically examine how news media constructs the societal implications of artificial intelligence. Through a qualitative analysis of 7 in 63 CNN news articles (2023–2025), the research reveals a dominant narrative that frames AI as an active, transformative agent, reinforcing a technological determinist ideology where human agency is marginalized. Appraisal analysis underscores a dual discourse: AI is simultaneously portrayed as an existential risk and an indispensable tool. Crucially, monoglossic engagement privileges elite voices while silencing affected communities, reproducing power asymmetries. The findings highlight media’s role in naturalizing corporate control over AI’s trajectory and advocate for inclusive narratives that center public agency. This study contributes to critical AI discourse analysis by exposing the linguistic mechanisms that shape public perception and legitimize inequitable technological futures.

  • SSCI Zhou, J. (under review). Agenda bundling and temporal drift: A multi-method sentiment analysis of AI coverage across mainstream and specialist news media. Digital Scholarship in the Humanities. Abstract ▾

    Institutionally distinct news outlets may construct fundamentally different evaluative narratives of the same emerging technology, yet the mechanisms underlying such divergence remain insufficiently understood. This study addresses the issue through a computational analysis of 596 AI-related news articles published by the Associated Press and Wired magazine between January 2020 and December 2025. Employing a most-different-systems design, the analysis triangulates three lexicon-based sentiment instruments, namely Bing, AFINN, and the NRC Emotion Lexicon, alongside independent cross-validation using CardiffNLP’s twitter-RoBERTa-base sentiment model, and integrates dictionary-based topic classification grounded in a five-category thematic typology. A significant outlet-by-period interaction revealed that both outlets registered near-neutral sentiment during peak hype before Wired recovered more sharply toward positivity during normalization than AP. Topic-level analysis demonstrated that the two outlets exhibited statistically indistinguishable sentiment within shared topic categories, with aggregate divergence fully attributable to differential agenda bundling. AP overrepresented regulatory and ethical coverage while Wired overrepresented technical progress. Cross-method triangulation across four sentiment instruments confirmed that these patterns were robust to measurement instrument choice. These findings reframe the conventional tone gap between mainstream and specialist technology journalism as an agenda gap, and suggest that divergent public understandings of AI emerge primarily through editorial selection rather than differential evaluative expression.

Research Interests

My work bridges critical discourse analysis with computational methods to investigate how language mediates societal attitudes toward AI.

Corpus-assisted Discourse Analysis

Integrating corpus linguistic techniques with CDA frameworks to empirically ground the analysis of ideological patterns in media and political discourse.

Computational Discourse Analysis

Applying NLP methods — sentiment analysis, topic modeling (STM), and transformer-based models — to large-scale text corpora for cross-method validation of discourse patterns.

Critical Discourse Analysis

Qualitative and mixed-methods discourse analysis drawing on Proximization Theory, Systemic-Functional Linguistics, and posthumanist approaches to language.

AI Ethics & Media Discourse

Examining how news media construct public understanding of artificial intelligence, including cross-national comparisons of AI policy and governance discourse.
